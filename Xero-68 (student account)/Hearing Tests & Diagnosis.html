Hearing Tests & Diagnosis

Hearing tests are essential for evaluating how well a person can hear. These tests help diagnose hearing loss and determine its type and severity. A hearing specialist, such as an audiologist, typically performs these tests.

1. Initial Consultation

The process begins with a review of medical history and a discussion of any symptoms, such as:

  • Difficulty understanding speech
  • Asking others to repeat themselves
  • Ringing in the ears (tinnitus)
  • Sudden or gradual hearing loss

2. Physical Ear Examination

The audiologist may inspect the ears using an otoscope to check for blockages, infections, or structural issues that could affect hearing.

3. Types of Hearing Tests

  • Pure Tone Audiometry: Measures the softest sounds a person can hear at different frequencies.
  • Speech Audiometry: Tests how well someone understands speech at various volumes.
  • Tympanometry: Examines the movement of the eardrum to detect middle ear problems.
  • Otoacoustic Emissions (OAE): Checks the function of the inner ear (cochlea).
  • Auditory Brainstem Response (ABR): Measures how the brain responds to sounds; often used for newborns or unresponsive patients.

4. Diagnosis

Based on the test results, the audiologist will determine:

  • The type of hearing loss (conductive, sensorineural, or mixed)
  • The severity (mild, moderate, severe, or profound)
  • The best course of action for treatment or management

5. Next Steps

Treatment options may include hearing aids, cochlear implants, medical treatment, or communication strategies. Regular follow-ups may also be recommended to monitor hearing health over time.
