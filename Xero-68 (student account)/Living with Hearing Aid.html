Living with Hearing Aids

Hearing aids can significantly improve the quality of life for people with hearing loss. However, adjusting to and using hearing aids effectively involves more than just wearing a device. This guide is designed to help teachers, adults, and caregivers understand what it’s like to live with hearing aids, and how to provide meaningful support.

1. What Are Hearing Aids?

Hearing aids are small electronic devices worn in or behind the ear. They amplify sounds to help people with hearing loss hear more clearly. They are customized for each individual’s type and degree of hearing loss.

  • Microphone: Picks up sound from the environment.
  • Amplifier: Increases the volume of sounds.
  • Speaker: Sends the amplified sounds into the ear.
  • Battery: Powers the device; may be disposable or rechargeable.

2. Types of Hearing Aids

  • Behind-the-Ear (BTE): Sits behind the ear with a tube going into the ear canal.
  • In-the-Ear (ITE): Fits completely inside the outer ear.
  • In-the-Canal (ITC) / Completely-in-the-Canal (CIC): Very small and fits deeper in the ear canal.
  • Receiver-in-Canal (RIC): Similar to BTE but with better sound quality and comfort.

3. Common Challenges of Living with Hearing Aids

While hearing aids can be life-changing, they also present challenges, including:

  • Background noise may still be overwhelming or distracting.
  • Feedback (whistling sound) if not properly fitted.
  • Initial discomfort or awkwardness while adjusting to amplified sound.
  • Battery issues or maintenance requirements.
  • Social stigma or self-consciousness, especially in children and teens.
Tip for Teachers and Colleagues: Always speak clearly and face the person using hearing aids. Avoid covering your mouth or speaking too fast.

4. Supporting Someone with Hearing Aids

Whether you are a teacher, coworker, or family member, support makes a big difference:

  • Be patient: Adjusting to hearing aids takes time. Don’t assume hearing is “perfect” now.
  • Use visual cues: Gestures, facial expressions, and written instructions help reinforce communication.
  • Provide a quiet environment: Reduce background noise where possible, especially during conversation.
  • Check in: Ask the individual if they are hearing and understanding clearly, and if any support is needed.
Classroom Tip: Use assistive listening devices like FM systems or sound-field systems to help students with hearing aids hear the teacher better.

5. Hearing Aids in the Workplace and Classroom

People using hearing aids may still need accommodations in group settings. Here’s how you can help:

  • Use captions for videos and presentations.
  • Allow extra processing time during discussions.
  • Encourage peer support and inclusive participation.
  • Assign seating that minimizes distractions and maximizes clarity of sound.

6. Daily Life with Hearing Aids

  • Cleaning: Hearing aids must be kept clean and dry to function properly.
  • Charging/Battery: Rechargeable aids should be charged nightly; disposable batteries should be changed as needed.
  • Storage: Store hearing aids in a safe, dry place when not in use.
  • Routine check-ups: Hearing aid users should see an audiologist regularly for adjustments and maintenance.

For Adults: Join support groups or hearing loss communities. Sharing experiences can ease anxiety and provide useful strategies.

7. Addressing Myths and Misconceptions

  • Myth: "Hearing aids restore normal hearing."
    Fact: They improve hearing, but not to the level of natural hearing in most cases.
  • Myth: "Only elderly people wear hearing aids."
    Fact: Hearing loss affects people of all ages—including children and young adults.
  • Myth: "People with hearing aids don’t need other help."
    Fact: They still benefit from visual support and communication strategies.

8. Encouraging Acceptance and Confidence

Normalize the use of hearing aids by talking about them openly and respectfully. Encourage users—especially students—to embrace their devices as tools that support their success. Create environments where it’s okay to ask for help or repetition without embarrassment.
